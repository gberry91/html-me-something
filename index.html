The Legend of Zelda

The Legend of Zelda is a video game franchise loved by millions of people around the world. Since the first game in the franchise was released on the Nintendo Famicom in 1986, the series has been a staple in the eyes of gamers and non-gamers alike.

NES Era

The release of the Famicom by Nintendo in 1983 saw the video game industry take hold of living rooms across Japan. After gaining critical and commercial success, Nintendo released an updated version of the console in North America two years later. Nintendo became an icon with their new mascot Super Mario, created by Shigeru Miyamoto. Seeing Mario as one dimensional and not capable of proving a completely unrestricted experience, Miyamoto went back to the drawing board and started work on his next game: The Legend of Zelda.

The Legend of Zelda was released in Japan on February 21, 1986. Selling over 6.5 million copies, The game was met with critical acclaim. Fans of the series were treated to a sequel with 1987's Zelda II: The Adventure of Link. Though it too gained critical and financial success, it as seen as the black sheep in the series by fans due to it's level of difficulty and side-scrolling battle mechanics.

SNES & the Handheld Market

With the release of a new console, the Super Famicom, Miyamoto and the staff at Nintendo began working on the second sequel to their 1986 hit. Released one year after the console hit stores, The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past was an instant classic. It focused more on storytelling and delivering a cohesive narrative rather than open world freedom. This would later become a point of contention among fans of the series as each subsequent game would progessively become more linear.

Due to the overwhelming success of A Link to the Past, the Zelda team began working on a port of the game to it's handheld game console, the Nintendo Gameboy. After several months of working on the game the project turned into an original story written by Yoshiaki Koizumi. The new mobile Zelda was released under the name Link's Awakening in June of 1993. The game was later remade in color for the Gameboy Color in 1998. Both copies like their home console veriosns sold exceptionally well and are still considered some of Nintendo's best games. Dude to the success of both versions of Link's Awakening, Nintendo would continue to develop 2D Zelda games on it's handheld consoles as home consoles transitioned into the world of 3D rendered graphics.

Ocarina of Time & the Transition to 3D

As the the home video game industry saw a boom in popularity and availability in the late 80s and early 90s, game devlopers were constantly competing with each other and rushing to come out with the next big thing. Nintendo was looking to their next console: the Nintendo 64. As the N64 was capable of rendering graphics in 3D, Miyomoto and his team began working on the first 3D Zelda. Using a similar narrative structure to A Link to the Past, the new Zelda game dubbed Ocarina of Time presented the Zelda's world with depth for the first time. As the camera perspective was changed from an overhead view to a behind the shoulder angle, developers ran into issues translating the action oriented sections of the game into 3D in a way that was comfortable and intuitive to the player. To make the combat work in a 3D environment the team introduced "Z-Targeting" as a way to track enemies around the 3D environment. This became a staple of the franchise, having been in every 3D Zelda game since. Ocarina of Time received universal praise from critics and gamers over the world selling over 7.6 million copies world-wide and is considered the one of the highest rated games of all time.

Ocarina of Time received a sequel in 2000 with the release of Majora's Mask'. Using the assets from it's predecessor, Majora's Mask was developed in just a year. Though it was not quite as successful as other titles in the series Majora is now considered a cult classic and has gained support from many as an essential game in the series and one of the best video game sequels ever created.

Post-Ocarina Games & the Future of Zelda

Following the transition to 3D, Nintendo released three more games in the series with The Windwaker, Twilight Princess, and Skyward Sword respectively. Though each game introduced a new gameplay mechanic with the implementation of key items and new methods of control input, the formula created in A Link to the Past and revolutionized in Ocarina of Time largely remained unchanged since the transition to 3D. As with previous games in the series, the later entries in the Zelda franchise received critical and commercial acclaim due to their innovation of the adventure genre and their impressive visual storytelling.

With the latest game in the franchise The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild, Miyamoto and the development team shifted their focus back to open word exploring with a lighter emphasis on direct storytelling. Becoming one of the highest selling and rated games of all time upon release, Breath of the Wild has continued the practice of introducing new game mechanics and innovation through cutting edge hardware and technology. Many gamers and fans eagerly wait to see what new ideas Nintendo will come up with in future installments.
